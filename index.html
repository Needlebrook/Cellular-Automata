About Cellular Automata
What are Cellular Automata?

Cellular automata are simulations made up of tiny squares called cells. Each cell can be either alive or dead (or sometimes have more than two states).

Time advances in steps called generations. At every generation, each cell looks at its neighbouring cells and follows a simple set of rules.

Although the rules are simple, they can create surprisingly complex behaviour including moving patterns, self-replication, maze formation, fractals, and structures that appear almost alive.

Use your mouse to draw cells on the grid, then press Play to watch the automaton evolve.

Understanding the rule notation

The format B/S describes how cells are born and survive.

  • B = Birth conditions
  • S = Survival conditions

For example, Conway's Life uses B3/S23. A dead cell becomes alive if it has exactly 3 neighbours, while a living cell survives with 2 or 3 neighbours.
